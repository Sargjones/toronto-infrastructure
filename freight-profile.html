Reference — Transport & Logistics

GTA Rail Freight Profile

The Greater Toronto Area is served by two Class I freight railways — CN and CPKC — whose combined network moves the goods that keep the region's economy, food supply, automotive sector, and critical infrastructure running. This page documents what moves through the GTA by rail, how quickly a disruption cascades into visible impacts, and why freight rail labour risk is a direct concern for Toronto resilience.

Major GTA rail terminals
CN Rail
Brampton Intermodal Terminal (BIT)
76 Intermodal Drive, Brampton. Canada's largest inland rail facility at 680 acres. 250,000 m² of paved yard, 10 loading tracks. Handles containerized freight, automotive parts, and bulk commodities with 24/7 operations. Connected to CN's MacMillan Yard (Toronto) via the Halton Subdivision. Processes ~8-9 trains per day. Canadian Tire's national distribution centre is an adjacent anchor client.
CPKC
Vaughan Intermodal Terminal
Located 10 km east of BIT, connecting CPKC's east-west and north-south corridors. Serves the Milton GO corridor (GO Transit shares CPKC track on the Milton line — 30,000 commuters affected directly when CPKC stopped in August 2024). Agincourt classification yard (Scarborough) handles bulk and mixed freight sorting for eastern Canada distribution.
CN Rail — Critical Infrastructure
Malport Yard & Jet Fuel Facility
Steeles Ave & Airport Rd, Mississauga. Smaller switching yard connected to BIT and MacMillan Yard. Adjacent to the Jet Fuel Rail Offloading, Storage and Distribution Facility — the primary fuel supply point for Pearson International Airport. Built at $59-65M, opened July 2009. Also handles dangerous goods including chlorine for Toronto Water treatment plants.
CN Rail
MacMillan Yard (Toronto)
Caledonia Rd & Keele St, Toronto. CN's primary Toronto classification yard, connecting the Oakville Subdivision (lakeshore corridor to Windsor) and Kingston Subdivision (corridor to Montreal). GO Transit Barrie, Kitchener, Stouffville, and Richmond Hill lines run on CN track through this corridor — meaning CN freight operations directly influence GO Transit schedules daily.
Commodity mix — GTA rail freight
Intermodal containers
22% of CN revenue
Consumer goods, Asian imports transloaded from Vancouver and Prince Rupert, retail merchandise for GTA distribution. CN's largest and fastest-growing segment. Over 60% of Ontario's warehousing and distribution centres are within 30km of Brampton BIT.
Petroleum & chemicals
~20% of revenue
Jet fuel to Pearson International via CN Malport facility, chlorine for Toronto Water treatment plants, LPG for residential areas without natural gas, industrial chemicals for manufacturing. The most critical segment for Toronto resilience — see cross-sector cascade below.
Automotive parts
just-in-time
Parts for the Windsor-Toronto-Oshawa automotive corridor: Ford Oakville Assembly, Stellantis Windsor, GM Oshawa. Plants carry 4-8 hours of parts inventory. A rail stoppage shuts assembly lines within one business day. Ontario automotive assembly generates ~$22B in GDP annually.
Food & perishables
daily volumes
California and Pacific Northwest produce, frozen goods, dairy distribution for ~6 million GTA residents. Intermodal refrigerated containers (reefers) move on the same trains as general merchandise.
Metals & minerals
industrial
Steel coil from Hamilton, aluminum, construction aggregates for GTA building sector. Longer supply chains mean impact from a rail stoppage takes weeks to show up on construction sites rather than days.
Dangerous goods
527K carloads/yr (CA)
Chlorine, propane, industrial acids, LPG, anhydrous ammonia. Regulated under Transport Canada's Transportation of Dangerous Goods Act. CN and CPKC conduct regular emergency response exercises at GTA yards. In 2016, CN and ERAC ran a full-scale exercise at Malport specifically around petroleum product handling.
⚠ Cross-sector cascade: CN Malport → Pearson Airport
CN's Malport Yard in Mississauga houses the Jet Fuel Rail Offloading, Storage and Distribution Facility, the primary supply point for jet fuel at Pearson International Airport. A CN labour stoppage is not solely a freight and commuter event — it is a direct threat to Pearson's fuel supply within 3-5 days as tank farm reserves deplete. This creates a live dependency between the CN Freight Labour Risk indicator and the Pearson Airport Operations indicator in TII. No other public Toronto infrastructure dashboard documents this dependency.

Similarly, chlorine and other water treatment chemicals arrive at Toronto Water's treatment plants by rail. A prolonged stoppage (beyond 7-14 days) would begin to affect water treatment operations as chemical reserves deplete — creating a second cross-sector cascade from Transport & Logistics into Water.

CN & CPKC labour dispute history — key dates
Aug 2024
First simultaneous CN and CPKC lockout — 9,300 workers affected
Federal government intervened with binding arbitration under Canada Labour Code s.107 within 17 hours. Full network recovery took several weeks. Under Canadian law, rail is not classified as an essential service.
2022
CN requests one-year contract extension; CPKC merger with KCS
Extension set up the 2024 simultaneous expiry. CPKC (Canadian Pacific + Kansas City Southern) became the first single-line railway connecting Canada, the US, and Mexico.
2019
CN national strike — engineers and conductors walk out
8-day stoppage. Back-to-work legislation introduced but not needed — settlement reached. Grain and propane supply chains most affected.
Pattern
Federal government has intervened in every significant CN or CP work stoppage since 2007
However, the 2024 stoppage demonstrated that even a 17-hour shutdown creates weeks of supply chain recovery. The risk is not a prolonged stoppage — it is the cascade from any stoppage of any duration.
