Fuel & Energy:
What's at Stake for Toronto

Toronto's fuel supply and electricity grid sit at the end of a long, international chain. Disruptions at Tier 1 — a war, a sanctions regime, a currency move — take weeks to reach the pump and months to resolve. This page explains the chain, the thresholds, and what elevated energy costs mean for the city.

The supply chain — Tier 1 → 2 → 3
Tier 1
Brent Crude Price (global commodity)
The international benchmark for ~2/3 of globally traded oil. Set in London based on North Sea production, but priced by global supply/demand including OPEC+ decisions, Middle East conflict, sanctions, and USD strength. A $10/bbl move in Brent flows through to every barrel processed by Ontario refineries.
↓ priced in USD, converted at CAD/USD rate
Tier 1
CAD/USD Exchange Rate
Canada buys crude in US dollars. A weaker loonie amplifies every crude price increase — when CAD trades at 1.37 instead of 1.25, the same barrel costs 10% more in Canadian dollars before any processing. In early 2026, both crude prices and a weak CAD are compressing simultaneously.
↓ refined at Sarnia; distributed via TCPL/Enbridge
Tier 2
TCPL Parkway & Enbridge Dawn (continental supply chain)
Natural gas for heating and electricity generation moves from western Canada through TransCanada's Mainline to the Parkway delivery point near Milton, then into the GTA distribution network. Dawn Hub (near Sarnia) is Canada's largest underground gas storage facility at ~284 Bcf capacity — the buffer that keeps Ontario warm when pipelines run tight. Elevated pipeline utilization at Parkway is a leading indicator of GTA supply stress.
↓ dispatched by IESO; refined at Sarnia/Imperial Oil
Tier 3
Toronto Fuel Price & IESO Generation (local delivery)
The pump price Torontonians pay reflects Tier 1 + Tier 2 pressures plus fixed taxes (~35–45¢/L regardless of crude). The IESO dispatches Ontario's grid in real time — when gas peaker plants run above 3,000 MW, it signals grid stress driven by upstream energy costs. These are the indicators residents and businesses feel directly.

What elevated fuel costs mean for Toronto systems
🚛 Logistics & food supply
Commercial trucking accounts for ~70% of food moved into Toronto. Diesel tracks crude closely. When fuel costs rise 20%, grocery delivery margins compress — costs pass through to shelf prices within 4–8 weeks. Toronto's grocery market is highly concentrated (Loblaw, Sobeys, Metro control ~70% of sales), limiting competitive pressure to absorb increases.
🏗 Construction sector
Heavy equipment, concrete mixing, and materials transport are fuel-intensive. With the Ontario HST rebate launching April 1, 2026 intended to stimulate 8,000 new housing starts, elevated diesel prices create a direct headwind — cost overruns on fuel reduce builder margins and can delay groundbreaking decisions.
🏠 Household heating
~70% of Toronto households heat with natural gas. Dawn Hub storage and TCPL Parkway utilization are the upstream signals. Winter 2026–27 supply security depends on storage levels entering October — below 150 Bcf is a warning, below 100 Bcf is a crisis. Summer injection season (April–October) is the window to rebuild reserves.
🚌 Transit & emergency services
The TTC operates ~2,000 buses, the majority diesel. Toronto EMS and Fire Services also maintain large diesel fleets. Elevated fuel costs increase operating budgets — in the context of a shelter system at 95% capacity and a new counter-terrorism unit requiring fleet deployments, energy cost pressure compounds existing budget stress at the City.
💡 Electricity prices
Ontario's baseload is nuclear (~55%) and hydro (~25%) — both largely insulated from fuel price swings. The risk is in gas peakers, dispatched during peak demand. High gas prices raise the marginal cost of peak power, flowing into the Global Adjustment charge on commercial/industrial electricity bills. Residential bills are partially buffered by regulated pricing.
📉 Economic downstream
Energy is embedded in nearly every cost structure. Sustained Brent above $100/bbl historically correlates with inflation re-acceleration, consumer spending pullback, and rising bankruptcy filings with a 6–12 month lag. TII's financial sector indicators — bankruptcy filings, unemployment, TRREB SNR — are the downstream signals to watch as energy stress propagates through the economy.
The tax floor — why pump prices don't fall as fast as crude

Toronto pump prices include ~35–45¢/L in fixed taxes that do not move with crude. This creates a floor: even if Brent fell to $40/bbl, Toronto pump prices would remain above 100¢/L. It also means tax policy can move the needle more immediately than market forces in either direction.

Fixed components (~35–45¢/L total)
Federal excise tax: 10¢/L
Ontario fuel tax: 14.7¢/L
HST (13% on total price): ~15–18¢/L at current prices
Note: federal carbon tax suspended April 1, 2025
Variable components (crude-linked)
Crude oil cost: ~55–65% of pre-tax price
Refinery margin: varies by season and utilization
Wholesale rack + retailer margin: 8–12¢/L typical
CAD/USD exchange rate: amplifies or dampens all crude moves
